Gas Laws — Knowledge Organiser

Pearson Edexcel International GCSE · Unit 6: Solids, Liquids and Gases

Core concept: kinetic particle theory of gases
Particle model

Gas particles are in continuous, rapid, random motion. They are widely spaced with negligible forces between them. When they collide with the walls of a container they exert a force per unit area — this is pressure.

Key quantities and units
Pressure (P)Force exerted by gas particles per unit area on container walls. Unit: Pa (pascals) or kPa
Volume (V)Space occupied by the gas. Unit: m³ or cm³ (1 m³ = 10⁶ cm³)
Temperature (T)Must always be in Kelvin (K) for gas law calculations. T(K) = T(°C) + 273
Amount of gas (n)Fixed amount of gas (constant) assumed in these laws
The three gas laws
Boyle's Law

Pressure and volume at constant temperature

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

As pressure increases, volume decreases proportionally. Inverse relationship.

Charles' Law

Volume and temperature at constant pressure

V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

As temperature increases, volume increases proportionally. Direct relationship.

Pressure Law

Pressure and temperature at constant volume

P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

As temperature increases, pressure increases proportionally. Direct relationship.

Combined gas law
All three variables change
P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

Used when pressure, volume and temperature all change. Temperature must be in Kelvin.

Particle explanations — why do the laws work?

Boyle's Law — particle explanation

If volume decreases at constant temperature, the same number of particles occupy a smaller space. Particles hit the walls more frequently → greater pressure.

Charles' Law — particle explanation

At higher temperature, particles move faster and hit walls harder. To keep pressure constant, the volume must expand until collision frequency is restored.

Pressure Law — particle explanation

At higher temperature in a fixed volume, particles move faster → hit walls harder and more often → pressure increases.

Absolute zero (0 K = −273 °C)

The temperature at which gas particles have minimum kinetic energy. In theory, gas volume and pressure would reach zero. Cannot go below 0 K.

Temperature conversion — critical rule
T(K) = T(°C) + 273

°C → Kelvin

T(°C) = T(K) − 273

Kelvin → °C

⚠ Always convert °C to K before substituting into any gas law equation.
